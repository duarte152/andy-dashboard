In that time, I have had an unusual vantage point: I can observe the same clinicians at month one, month six, and year two of using the same system — and the trajectory is rarely what anyone predicts.

Your 2025 systematic review in NPJ Digital Medicine on clinicians' acceptance and use of clinical decision support systems over time captures a pattern I have seen repeatedly in practice. The most important word in your title is *over time*. Laudite is not a CDSS in the traditional sense — it is a generative AI platform for medical reporting, used by over 3,500 clinicians in Brazil — but the adoption dynamics I observe closely mirror those you describe. There is typically early enthusiasm, then a plateau or even regression as novelty fades and cognitive load accumulates, followed — in the cases where we intervene well — by genuine, sustained integration. In the cases where we do not, the tool becomes shelfware. The system works. The adoption does not.

What I find missing in most digital health research — and what I could contribute to your group — is a rigorous examination of what happens in the *human infrastructure* surrounding a clinical AI tool: the onboarding design, the ongoing enablement programs, the moments of re-engagement after early disengagement. I have been building and iterating on exactly these interventions for four years, and I believe the question of how structured enablement programs affect the shape of the long-term adoption curve is both academically novel and practically consequential.

I have been thinking around questions of how structured enablement interventions shape the long-term adoption trajectory of AI-assisted clinical tools — but I would be grateful to be guided by your expertise on where the most relevant directions lie within your current work.
