## 5 Pilares — Textos Base

### 1. EXCEPTIONAL CHARACTER
*Excelência, trabalho duro, serviço, aprendizado contínuo, integridade, empatia*

> I have always been drawn to environments where there is no room for pretense — where the gap between what you say and what you do becomes visible immediately. The Brazilian Army was the first of these. At twenty, commanding officers twice my age across thirteen teams in two states, I learned that character is not what you claim in a stable moment — it is how you behave when the decision is consequential and the information is incomplete. That lesson followed me out of uniform and into entrepreneurship, where I co-founded Dream Bots as a university student, building automation solutions without external funding, without guarantees, and without the safety of a salary. It followed me into Laudite, where I spent four years growing from marketing intern to Product Enablement Lead — not because I chased promotions, but because I kept finding problems that needed solving and solving them. I do not know how to separate work from purpose. I have not needed to.

---

### 2. COMMUNITY ENGAGEMENT
*Trabalho colaborativo em desafios comunitários*

> The community I serve is not a neighborhood or a nonprofit — it is the 200,000 healthcare providers in Brazil navigating a digital health transformation that was designed around systems, not around people. Every week at Laudite, I sit with radiologists, pathologists, and endoscopists trying to build a clinical workflow that technology promised to simplify and that, too often, complicated instead. My work — designing the onboarding flows, the training programs, the enablement systems that help providers actually use the tools meant to help them — is community work in the truest sense: meeting people where they are, removing barriers that no one else was paid to remove, and measuring success by whether the technology serves the person, not just the company. I expanded our product's reach to new medical specialties not because it grew revenue (though it did), but because those providers needed tools built for how they actually work.

---

### 3. LEADERSHIP POTENTIAL
*Liderança de outros em direção a mudanças significativas*

> I have led in three registers, and each one taught me something the others could not. The Army taught me command leadership: the discipline of preparing systems — and the people inside them — to perform under uncertainty. Dream Bots taught me leadership by conviction: when no one reports to you, influence is earned entirely through vision, credibility, and showing up consistently. Laudite has taught me systemic leadership: how to change behavior at scale by designing the conditions in which people succeed, rather than supervising the people themselves. Today, I lead a team of product specialists who are, in effect, change agents — people whose job is to make a technology transformation feel possible for healthcare professionals who did not ask for it and do not have time for it. The progress I care about is not the metric. It is the moment a provider who was struggling tells me: "I finally get it."

---

### 4. ENTREPRENEURIAL SPIRIT
*Identificar necessidades e criar soluções criativas*

> I co-founded Dream Bots in my third year of university because I noticed a specific gap: small and medium businesses in Brazil were paying for generic software solutions that required customization they could never afford. The problem was not the software — it was the fit between tool and context. That framing stayed with me. At the START Global Fellowship — a program at the University of St. Gallen in Switzerland focused on scaling startups and impactful entrepreneurship — I worked alongside founders from Berlin, Singapore, and São Paulo to transform a concept into a viable MVP inside a structured, demanding innovation environment. It was my first real exposure to what entrepreneurship looked like when it crossed borders, industries, and assumptions. At Laudite, I applied the same lens: the enablement programs I built were not processes copied from a playbook — they were products, designed from customer research, iterated from feedback, and measured by outcomes. Entrepreneurship, for me, is not a job title. It is a way of seeing problems.

---

### 5. ACADEMIC STRENGTH
*Curiosidade intelectual alinhada ao programa*

> My academic interest is not abstract. I want to understand, rigorously, why health technology so often fails to deliver on its potential — not because of engineering failures, but because of organizational, behavioral, and policy failures that the engineers were never asked to solve. Four years inside a HealthTech company have given me the practitioner's view: I have watched AI-assisted tools fail at adoption despite flawless engineering. I have watched simpler products transform clinical workflows because someone invested in the human layer around them. What I lack is the theoretical scaffolding — the implementation science, the organizational behavior frameworks, the health policy perspectives — that would let me generalize from what I have seen into prescriptions that could work at system scale. The Master of Management at McGill is the environment where that scaffolding gets built: rigorous, interdisciplinary, designed for practitioners who already know what they are trying to solve.
