## Personal Statement — Draft 1

> **Prompt de referência (KHS):** *Share something about who you are, your achievements, and your future goals. Feel free to discuss what shaped you, what matters to you, and how your experiences connect to where you want to go.*

---

The first team I ever led was not mine to choose.

In 2019, as a Second Lieutenant in the Brazilian Army, I was assigned to supervise thirteen inspection teams operating across two states — Rio de Janeiro and Espírito Santo — under time pressure, with incomplete information and no margin for error. I was twenty-three years old. No one asked whether I felt ready. The situation simply required leadership, and I learned — quickly — that leadership is not about certainty. It is about deciding with what you have, and taking responsibility for what happens next.

That lesson has shaped every significant thing I have done since.

---

When I left the Army, I co-founded Dream Bots, an automation startup building tools for investment management, marketing, and customer engagement. The company grew out of a project I developed during the START Global Fellowship at the University of St. Gallen — a programme that placed me alongside two hundred early-stage founders from across Europe, Asia, and the Americas. St. Gallen taught me something that the Army had not: that the hardest problems are not logistical. They are institutional. Getting people to adopt a new system — whether a military protocol or a software product — requires understanding why change is threatening, not just why it is necessary.

Dream Bots helped me practice that understanding at scale. It also taught me, through the honest experience of building and shutting down a venture, that impact is not a product feature. It is an organisational design question.

---

That insight is what brought me to Laudite.

For the past four years, I have worked in HealthTech SaaS — most recently as Product Enablement Lead at Laudite, a company building clinical imaging and decision support software for Brazilian hospitals. My work sits precisely at the intersection that most technology implementations fail: the gap between what a system can do and what clinicians will actually use. I manage relationships with medical directors, radiologists, pathologists, and hospital administrators who rarely share a common vocabulary — and I am responsible for making sure that the technology closes that gap rather than widening it.

The scale of that challenge is not small. Brazil's health system treats over 200 million people, and the clinical decision support tools that could most improve outcomes are consistently underutilised — not because they are technically insufficient, but because adoption is treated as an afterthought. I have seen this pattern across dozens of hospital deployments. And I have become convinced that solving it is one of the most consequential design problems in global health today.

---

Stanford's MS&E programme — specifically the Health Systems Modeling and Technology & Engineering Management tracks — is where I want to build the analytical framework that this challenge requires. The academic work being done at Stanford on implementation science, health systems design, and technology-driven organisational change is directly applicable to what I am trying to solve. I want to work inside that intellectual environment — not as an observer, but as a researcher and practitioner who brings a specific problem from the field.

The Knight-Hennessy community matters to this goal in a precise way. The decisions that will shape how Brazil deploys clinical AI over the next decade will be made by people — in government, in hospitals, in startups — who need to have had this conversation at a serious level. I intend to be one of those people. And the Scholars I would work alongside at Stanford — people building systems, leading institutions, and navigating the same intersection of technology and public good in other contexts — are exactly the network through which that kind of work happens.

My goal is not to carry a credential back to Brazil. It is to carry a system of thinking — and a community of people who know how to build things that last.
