## ESSAY 2 — Networking (383 palavras)

> **Prompt oficial:** *Chevening Scholarships are awarded to individuals who will be future leaders or influencers in their home countries. Explain how you meet this requirement through networking, using clear examples of how you have built and used networks in the past and how you will use the Chevening network if you are successful.*

---

The most valuable professional relationship I have built in the past four years did not come from a conference badge or a LinkedIn request. It came from spending six months in 2021 trying to solve a problem no one had the answer to.

During the START Global Fellowship at the University of St. Gallen, I worked alongside 200 early-stage founders and investors from across the world. The programme did not teach me to collect contacts — it taught me that a network is built through usefulness. I arrived with a concept for Dream Bots and left with something more durable: relationships with people who had shipped products and failed and rebuilt. An advisor I met there introduced me to a client who became our first anchor account in Brazil. Another connection helped me understand how automation was being regulated in Europe six months before those regulations reached our market.

That principle — networks grow through exchange, not collection — shapes how I operate at Laudite. As Product Enablement Lead, I manage relationships across clinical teams, hospital administrators, and engineers who rarely speak the same language. I have built trust with medical directors by solving problems they had never been able to articulate to a technology vendor. Those relationships are not contacts — they are the infrastructure through which clinical technology actually gets used.

As a Chevening Scholar, I intend to apply the same approach to the UK's research and innovation ecosystem. My immediate goal is to connect the people I will meet — at UCL's Global Business School for Health, at Imperial's Innovation & Entrepreneurship community, and through the Chevening alumni network — with the Brazilian HealthTech sector I know from the inside. Brazil is building its Rede Nacional de Dados em Saúde, a national health data infrastructure that will reshape clinical decision-making across the country. British researchers at the frontier of Learning Health Systems and clinical AI implementation could find genuine research partners there. I intend to be the bridge.

I also plan to contribute actively to the Chevening Brazil Alumni Network — not only as a recipient of its mentorship but as someone who helps the next cohort of Brazilian health innovators navigate the UK ecosystem.

That is what a network is for.

## ESSAY 3 — Studying in the UK (407 palavras)

> **Prompt oficial:** *Please explain why you have chosen your three university courses. Why do you want to study in the UK rather than in your home country or another country? How does your proposed course of study relate to your professional background?*

---

Three programmes. Three angles on the same question: how do you build digital health organisations that generate lasting clinical impact?

My first choice is the UCL MSc in Digital Health and Entrepreneurship. The Global Business School for Health — the first business school in the world designed explicitly around health as a market — is precisely the frame I need. Modules on Health Technology Assessment, Digital Health Business Models, and AI in Health map directly onto the decisions I make daily at Laudite. I have also been in contact with Dr. Watjana Lilaonitkul at UCL's Institute of Health Informatics, whose research on clinical decision support and responsible AI deployment extends the practical questions I work on into rigorous academic territory. UCL would give me both the business and the research depth to work on these problems at a different level.

My second choice is the Imperial College London MSc in Global Health Management (Innovation & Entrepreneurship track). Imperial offers something different: an institution where a business school shares a campus with world-leading engineering and medical faculties. I have been in contact with Professor Brendan Delaney at the Institute of Global Health Innovation, whose work on Learning Health Systems addresses exactly the gap I observe between clinical AI development and its operational deployment in practice. Imperial's programme would give me the systems-level framework I need to address that problem at scale.

My third choice is the University of Manchester MSc in Innovation Management and Entrepreneurship. Manchester's Masood Entrepreneurship Centre and its direct ties to the region's industrial and technology ecosystem offer a perspective that broadens beyond digital health into the wider dynamics of technology-driven organisational change — which I believe is essential background for anyone who wants to lead transformation, not just participate in it.

The UK is not an arbitrary choice. The NHS is the world's largest single-payer health system — a singular laboratory for studying what happens when digital tools meet institutional scale. The research being conducted at UCL, Imperial, and Edinburgh on clinical AI adoption, health informatics, and implementation science does not happen at this depth anywhere else in the world. I want to be inside that conversation — not studying it from a distance, but working within the institutions that are shaping how it develops.

Brazil will need people who have done that. I intend to be one of them.

## ESSAY 4 — Career Plans (391 palavras)

> **Prompt oficial:** *What are your plans for your career after completing your Chevening Scholarship? Please describe your short-term plans (1-2 years after study), medium-term plans (3-5 years after returning home) and long-term plans (5+ years). How will the Chevening Scholarship help you to achieve these goals?*

---

I do not want to manage a product. I want to build a system.

In the short term — the two years after returning from the UK — I will move into a senior product strategy or innovation leadership role within the Brazilian HealthTech sector. Whether that means joining a scale-up like Alice Health or Nilo Saúde, expanding my own previous venture (Dream Bots) into clinical AI enablement, or leading product strategy at a hospital system investing in digital transformation, the objective is the same: to translate what I learn in the UK into measurable impact in one of the largest and fastest-growing healthcare markets in Latin America.

In the medium term — three to five years out — I want to build the bridge I can already see from both sides. Brazil's Rede Nacional de Dados em Saúde (RNDS), the national health data infrastructure now being deployed, is creating conditions for AI-powered clinical decision support at a scale that few countries have attempted. British researchers are producing the frameworks — on implementation science, Learning Health Systems, and responsible AI governance — that Brazil will need to make that work in practice. My goal is to actively connect those two worlds: as an advisor, collaborator, and translator between the UK academic ecosystem I will have been part of and the Brazilian health innovation ecosystem I come from. The Chevening alumni network gives me the institutional structure to do that with continuity.

In the long term — beyond five years — I want to have meaningful influence on how Brazil governs and deploys health data at a national level. That may mean advising regulatory bodies like ANS or contributing to the Ministry of Health's digital health strategy. It may mean founding an organisation that trains clinical product leaders for the Brazilian market. But the direction is clear: I want to help build the infrastructure that makes better clinical decisions accessible to more Brazilians — not as a feature, but as a system.

The Chevening Scholarship is the specific investment that makes this trajectory possible. The combination of a world-class academic environment, a network of alumni who have made this return journey before, and the institutional credibility that comes with it will shape how I work in Brazil for the next decade.
