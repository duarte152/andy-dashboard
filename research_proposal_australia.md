## TÍTULO (versão base)

**Beyond Deployment: How Structured Enablement Programs Shape the Long-Term Adoption Trajectory of Clinical Decision Support Systems**

---

## 1. INTRODUCTION AND RESEARCH PROBLEM

Healthcare organisations globally are investing in clinical decision support systems (CDSS) and digital health tools at unprecedented scale. Yet evidence consistently shows that initial deployment does not translate into sustained clinical use. Studies report that between 30% and 70% of health IT implementations fail to achieve the adoption levels necessary to generate clinical benefit - not because of technological shortcomings, but because the organisational, behavioural, and educational conditions required to sustain use are rarely adequately designed or resourced.

This research problem is not merely academic. I have observed it directly over four years as Product Specialists & Enablement Lead at Laudite, a HealthTech SaaS company operating in the medical imaging and clinical reporting sector in Brazil. My role has been to design and deliver the enablement infrastructure - onboarding programmes, training systems, re-engagement interventions, and workflow integration support - that determines whether clinicians move from initial use to genuine integration of digital tools. What I have observed is a consistent pattern: adoption does not follow a linear upward curve. There is typically initial engagement, a plateau or regression as cognitive load accumulates and early support structures fade, and then - in cases where structured enablement continues - a second phase of deeper, more durable adoption. In cases where enablement is discontinued, tools become underused or abandoned despite demonstrated clinical efficacy.

This observation frames the central question this research seeks to address.

---

## 2. RESEARCH QUESTIONS

**Primary question:**
How do structured enablement programmes - specifically their design, timing, intensity, and continuity - influence the shape of the long-term adoption trajectory of clinical decision support systems in hospital settings?

**Secondary questions:**
1. What are the critical points in the adoption trajectory at which structured enablement has the greatest effect on sustained use?
2. What programme design characteristics (content, delivery format, frequency, duration) are most strongly associated with durable adoption outcomes?
3. How do contextual factors - including clinical specialty, organisational culture, and technology design - moderate the relationship between enablement interventions and adoption trajectories?

---

## 3. THEORETICAL FRAMEWORK

This research draws on three converging theoretical traditions:

**Technology Acceptance and Post-Acceptance Models:** While TAM and its successors (UTAUT, UTAUT2) have been extensively applied to initial adoption decisions, their application to long-term, dynamic adoption trajectories - including post-initial-use regression and re-adoption - remains limited. This research contributes to extending post-adoption frameworks to account for the role of structured interventions.

**Implementation Science:** The Consolidated Framework for Implementation Research (CFIR), the Implementation Outcomes Framework (Proctor et al., 2011), and related models provide tools for analysing the organisational and contextual factors that moderate implementation success. This research applies these frameworks specifically to the question of enablement as an implementation strategy.

**Organisational Learning and Capability Building:** Drawing on concepts of absorptive capacity and training-to-transfer research from organisational learning theory, this research analyses enablement programmes as deliberate organisational investments in building the capabilities required to extract value from digital tools.

## 4. METHODOLOGY

**Research design:** Mixed-methods longitudinal study

**Phase 1 - Systematic review (Months 1–4)**
A systematic review of empirical studies examining post-adoption CDSS use trajectories and the interventions designed to support them. Databases: PubMed, CINAHL, Scopus, ACM Digital Library. Focus on studies reporting longitudinal adoption data with a minimum 12-month follow-up period.

**Phase 2 - Qualitative case studies (Months 4–10)**
Semi-structured interviews and document analysis across 3–4 healthcare organisations that have implemented CDSS tools within the past 3 years, with varying levels of structured enablement investment. Participants: product enablement professionals, clinical informatics leads, medical directors, and frontline clinicians. Analysis: thematic analysis using NVivo; framework analysis mapping to CFIR constructs.

**Phase 3 - Survey study (Months 8–14)**
Cross-sectional survey of clinicians at organisations with active CDSS deployments, measuring current adoption stage, enablement programme exposure, and self-reported competency and workflow integration. Validated scales: UTAUT2 (behavioural intention and use behaviour), Kirkpatrick Level 3 (transfer of training), organisational support measures. Analysis: structural equation modelling (SEM) to test mediation hypotheses.

**Phase 4 - Integration and theoretical development (Months 12–18)**
Integration of qualitative and quantitative findings using joint display analysis. Development of a provisional framework for enablement programme design in CDSS deployment contexts, with practical implications for health IT implementation practitioners.

## 5. SIGNIFICANCE AND CONTRIBUTION

**Theoretical contribution:**
This research extends adoption and implementation frameworks by: (a) modelling adoption as a dynamic, non-linear trajectory rather than a binary state; and (b) theorising structured enablement as a category of implementation strategy with identifiable design parameters and measurable longitudinal effects. The resulting framework will offer a more realistic and actionable model of how clinical technology adoption can be designed and sustained.

**Practical contribution:**
The proposed framework will provide evidence-based guidance for HealthTech companies and healthcare organisations on how to design, resource, and time enablement programmes to maximise adoption outcomes. In a sector where implementation failure carries direct clinical costs, this contribution has immediate application. The findings will be particularly relevant to contexts like Brazil, where the national health data infrastructure (Rede Nacional de Dados em Saúde, RNDS) is currently being deployed across thousands of healthcare providers - generating an unprecedented implementation challenge with limited enablement infrastructure.

**Methodological contribution:**
By combining longitudinal qualitative case studies with cross-sectional survey modelling, this research demonstrates an integrated mixed-methods approach to studying complex, time-sensitive organisational phenomena in clinical settings.
