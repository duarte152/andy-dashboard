## Rascunho (497 palavras)

The moment I understood what leadership truly means, I was twenty-two years old, standing before thirteen soldiers waiting for orders in conditions designed to test every limit I had. As a Lieutenant in the Brazilian Army, I did not inherit authority — I earned it, day by day, through presence, accountability, and the willingness to be wrong in front of the people I led.

That experience shaped everything that came after.

When I left the Army and co-founded Dream Bots in 2021, I carried with me a principle I had learned in the field: a team executes not because it is told to, but because it understands *why*. At Dream Bots, I built our automation practice from zero — recruiting, defining the technical roadmap, selling to clients, and managing delivery simultaneously. For three years, I led a team navigating the specific uncertainty of an early-stage startup: unclear market, limited resources, and the constant need to make decisions with incomplete information. We grew the company to a point where it was generating real impact for clients — and I learned that leadership in a startup is not about having answers. It is about creating the conditions where good answers can emerge.

That lesson deepened at Laudite. As Product Enablement Lead at a HealthTech SaaS company focused on medical imaging, I work at the intersection of clinical teams, engineers, and hospital administrators — three groups with fundamentally different languages, incentive structures, and definitions of success. My role is not to manage any of them directly. It is to align them. Every product adoption we achieve in a hospital happens because I was able to translate clinical needs into product requirements, build trust with medical directors, and help clinicians see how a technology decision supports — rather than disrupts — their practice. This is the kind of influence I believe matters most: the kind that moves systems, not just individuals.

My participation in the START Global Fellowship program at the University of St. Gallen in Switzerland reinforced this conviction. Surrounded by 200 young entrepreneurs and innovators from across the world, I was exposed to leadership models I had never encountered — decentralised, values-driven, globally-minded. I returned to Brazil with a clearer sense of the leader I want to become: one who can operate across borders, bridge health systems and technology, and contribute to building the infrastructure that makes better clinical decisions possible for more people.

The UK is where that journey continues. Digital health is not a future sector in Britain — it is a present reality, being shaped right now inside the NHS, inside research labs at UCL and Imperial, inside policy rooms at NHSX. I want to be part of that conversation. And I want to bring it back to Brazil.

Leadership, for me, has never been about the title. It has always been about the responsibility to move something important forward — even when the path is not yet clear.
