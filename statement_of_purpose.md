# Statement of Purpose — Base Version
## Thiago Rocha Duarte

*~800 words | Adaptable for: Technology Management, Digital Health, Innovation, Entrepreneurship programs*

The moment I understood that technology could save lives — not metaphorically, but literally — was not in a classroom. It was on the floor of a HealthTech startup, watching a healthcare provider struggle to navigate a product that held the clinical workflow for dozens of patients. The system was powerful. The user was lost. In the gap between technological capability and human understanding, I found my life's work — and the question that drives me toward graduate study: how do we build, scale, and govern technology that genuinely serves people?

My path to that question has been anything but linear. It began in the structured world of the Brazilian Army, where at twenty years old I was commissioned as a Second Lieutenant and handed responsibility for thirteen teams across two states. The military gave me something that no textbook can: the experience of leading under pressure, making consequential decisions with incomplete information, and understanding that systems only function when the people inside them are properly equipped. I carried those lessons out of uniform and into the world of technology, where they proved more relevant than I anticipated.

While completing my degree at PUC-Rio, I co-founded Dream Bots, an automation startup building solutions for investment operations, marketing, and customer service. Running a company as a student taught me the brutal, clarifying lessons of entrepreneurship: ideas are cheap, execution is everything, and the distance between an MVP and a product people actually use is measured in conversations with customers, not lines of code. That conviction deepened when I was selected for the START Global Fellowship program at the University of St. Gallen in Switzerland — a remote program focused on scaling startups and impactful entrepreneurship, bringing together early-stage founders from around the world. Working within that ecosystem, transforming a concept into a viable product alongside peers from Berlin, Singapore, and São Paulo, gave me my first taste of international innovation culture and confirmed that my intellectual home sits at the intersection of technology, entrepreneurship, and impact. It also made one thing clear: the most interesting problems in technology do not respect national borders, and solving them requires a perspective as global as the systems they address.

After university, I joined Laudite — a HealthTech SaaS company improving clinical and administrative workflows in healthcare. What began as a marketing internship evolved, over four years, into something I had not anticipated: a complete view of how a technology product lives or dies in the real world. As a Customer Success Analyst, I sat at the table where product promise met operational reality, translating healthcare professionals' frustrations into product insights. As a Product Specialist, I became the bridge between engineering capabilities and the needs of providers who had neither the time nor the inclination to learn new software from scratch. Today, as Product Specialists & Enablement Lead, I design the systems by which an organization learns to use its own product — the training programs, the playbooks, the onboarding flows, the videos that turn confusion into confidence.

Healthcare is a particularly unforgiving environment for testing these ideas. The margin for error is narrow, the stakeholders are simultaneously sophisticated and time-starved, and the consequences of poor technology adoption are not measured in churn rates but in clinical outcomes. Four years inside this space have given me a granular understanding of why digital health transformation so often stalls — not because the technology is inadequate, but because the organizational, behavioral, and design challenges surrounding it are routinely underestimated. I have watched AI-assisted tools fail at adoption despite flawless engineering. I have watched simpler products transform workflows because someone invested in the human layer around them.

This is where my academic ambitions take shape. I want to study, rigorously, the conditions under which health technology actually gets adopted — at scale, across diverse healthcare systems. I am drawn to research at the intersection of technology management, implementation science, and organizational behavior. I want to engage with the policy and governance questions that will determine whether digital health and AI in medicine deliver on their extraordinary potential or deepen existing inequalities.

A fully funded international master's program is the logical next step in this trajectory. I have spent four years building practitioner expertise in a domain that moves faster than any single industry can process alone. I need the intellectual frameworks, research methodologies, and cross-disciplinary exposure that only a rigorous academic environment provides. I want to sit in rooms with economists studying healthcare markets, computer scientists designing clinical AI, and public health researchers mapping adoption barriers in low-resource settings — and bring to those conversations what the industry rarely does: the lived experience of watching a technology product succeed, fail, and find its footing inside a real healthcare organization.

The military taught me that leadership means preparing systems — and the people inside them — to perform under uncertainty. Entrepreneurship taught me that the best solutions come from obsessive focus on the human beings a product is meant to serve. HealthTech taught me that the gap between what technology can do and what it actually does for people is the most consequential gap in modern healthcare. Graduate study is where I intend to close it — not for the credential, but for the tools to work on a problem that genuinely matters.
