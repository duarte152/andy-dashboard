Dear Professor Baysari,

I am writing to explore the possibility of pursuing a Research Master's degree under your supervision at the University of Sydney, with a potential start in 2027.

My name is Thiago Rocha Duarte - a Brazilian and Portuguese national, and currently Product Specialists & Product Enablement Lead at Laudite, a HealthTech SaaS company operating in the medical imaging and clinical reporting space. Over four years, I have worked directly with radiologists, pathologists, and endoscopists as they navigate new digital workflows. In that time, I have had an unusual vantage point: I can observe the same clinicians at month one, month six, and year two of using the same system - and the trajectory is rarely what anyone predicts.

Your 2025 systematic review in NPJ Digital Medicine on clinicians' acceptance and use of clinical decision support systems over time captures patterns I have observed directly in practice. Laudite is not a CDSS in the traditional sense - it is a generative AI platform for medical reporting, used by over 3,500 clinicians in Brazil - but the adoption dynamics closely mirror what you describe, with some nuances I rarely see discussed in the literature.

The starting condition matters enormously: when a clinician is the agent of their own adoption, there is genuine early motivation; when the tool is mandated by management or hospital administration, the initial response is often resistance. The pivotal moment in either case is what we call "first value" - the first time a clinician completes a real workflow cycle with the new system and discovers it actually works - a milestone we monitor through a "time to first value" metric. That moment dissolves the fear of transition: concerns about productivity loss, about AI-generated content introducing errors, about losing quality control. Once that threshold is crossed, clinicians typically find their own optimised rhythm within one to two months, and satisfaction rises sharply. What complicates the picture further is accumulated intellectual capital - clinicians who have invested years building templates, macros, or structured content in a previous system face an additional switching cost that purely technical evaluations of adoption almost never account for.

What I find missing in most digital health research - and what I could contribute to your group - is a rigorous examination of what happens in the *human infrastructure* surrounding a clinical AI tool: the onboarding design, the ongoing enablement programs, the moments of re-engagement after early disengagement. I have been building and iterating on exactly these interventions for four years, and I believe the question of how structured enablement programs affect the shape of the long-term adoption curve remains underexplored in the literature - particularly for systems centred around generative AI and other recently developed clinical AI technologies.

I have been thinking around questions of how structured enablement interventions shape the long-term adoption trajectory of AI-assisted clinical tools - but I would be grateful to be guided by your expertise on where the most relevant directions lie within your current work.
