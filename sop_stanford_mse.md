---

Every clinical deployment I have managed at Laudite begins with the same conversation. A radiologist, a pathologist, or a gastroenterologist sits across from me in a hospital meeting room and explains, with varying degrees of patience, why the system we are implementing will not work. Sometimes they are wrong about the specific objection. Most of the time, they are right about the underlying problem: the technology was designed without them, and no one thought seriously about how it would fit into their practice.

I have spent four years trying to close that gap. The problem has not gotten easier. It has become clearer.

---

I joined Laudite in 2022 as a Customer Success Analyst in a HealthTech SaaS company focused on medical imaging and clinical decision support software (CDSS) for Brazilian hospitals. Over four years — progressing to Product Enablement Lead — I have been responsible for the full arc of product adoption: from initial discovery with clinical teams, through go-to-market strategy, onboarding, and long-term enablement across specialties that include radiology, pathology, and endoscopy. I have worked with medical directors who embrace digital tools and with clinicians who reject them after three days. I have shipped features that improved adoption rates and watched technically superior products fail because the workflow integration was wrong.

The pattern I keep encountering is not a technology problem. It is a design problem — and underneath it, a knowledge problem. We do not have good models for how organisational structure, clinical workflow design, and trust dynamics interact to determine whether a CDSS is used continuously, used occasionally, or abandoned. The literature on technology acceptance in healthcare is rich but largely cross-sectional; it describes what clinicians say they will use, not what they actually use over time, and rarely under conditions that resemble real-world hospital deployments in resource-constrained systems.

Brazil makes this problem unusually legible. With over 200 million people, a fragmented public-private health system, enormous institutional variation across geographies, and a growing HealthTech sector deploying tools at scale, Brazil offers a natural laboratory for studying CDSS adoption under heterogeneous conditions. The same tool behaves differently in a large academic hospital in São Paulo and a mid-complexity regional hospital in the interior. Understanding why — and building models that can predict and improve adoption across that variation — is the research question I want to spend the next five years answering.

---

My research agenda is organised around three interconnected questions:

**1. What organisational and workflow factors predict sustained CDSS use versus early abandonment?**
Drawing on longitudinal deployment data from real hospital environments, I want to build quantitative models that separate the signal from noise: which variables — team size, clinical role distribution, EHR integration quality, IT support capacity, training modality — actually predict whether a tool is being used twelve months after go-live? This requires both the modelling toolkit of management science and engineering and the domain knowledge to design the right measurement instruments and interpret the results correctly.

**2. How does the design of the enablement process affect adoption outcomes?**
In the commercial HealthTech world, "enablement" is treated as a cost centre, not a research question. I believe it is one of the most consequential intervention points in the CDSS deployment chain. I want to study enablement as an independent variable: comparing structured onboarding models, peer-learning approaches, and AI-assisted training workflows in controlled deployment environments.

**3. How do findings from Brazilian hospital deployments generalise — and where do they not?**
Brazil's health system combines features found in both high-income and lower-middle-income contexts. Research conducted here has potential relevance for digital health implementation challenges globally. I want to build a framework that explicitly addresses generalisability from the design phase.

---

Stanford's MS&E programme is the right home for this research. The Health Systems Modeling track gives me access to the quantitative methods this work demands — optimisation, stochastic modelling, discrete event simulation — without separating me from the clinical and organisational context that makes those methods meaningful. The Technology & Engineering Management track offers complementary grounding in how organisations adopt and resist innovation.

I am particularly interested in working with Professor David Scheinker, whose SURF Stanford Medicine initiative models exactly the research practice I want to develop: interdisciplinary teams embedded in hospital environments, solving operational problems with direct clinical consequence. His work on remote patient monitoring algorithms and digital health equity addresses the same design-for-adoption questions that animate my own research. I am also drawn to the work of Professor Arnold Milstein at the Clinical Excellence Research Center, whose research on AI-assisted clinical decision support — including the recent Nature Medicine RCT on GPT-4 in clinical practice — represents the kind of evidence-based implementation science I want to build on.

My application to the Knight-Hennessy Scholars programme is an integral part of this plan. The KHS community is not simply a funding mechanism; it is a peer network of people who are thinking about how to make institutions work better for the populations they serve. That conversation — across sectors, disciplines, and geographies — is one that I believe will shape how Brazil's health system navigates its next decade of technological transformation. I intend to contribute to it, and to bring what I learn back.

---

I am twenty-eight years old. I have led military teams under operational pressure, built and wound down a startup, and spent four years translating technology into clinical practice. The thread connecting those experiences is a persistent, specific question: what does it actually take to make a new system work inside a human institution? Stanford is where I want to pursue that question at the level of rigour it deserves.
